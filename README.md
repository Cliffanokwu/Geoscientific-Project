Sediment waves are important geomorphic features that provide valuable insights into the dynamics of deep-water bottom currents and sediment transport processes. This study investigates the morphology of sediment waves in the Argentine Basin and the Hatteras Outer Ridge morphometric measurements. Morphological parameters were analysed to classify sediment waves and infer bottom-current characteristics across the study areas.
Three groups of sediment waves were identified: small-scale waves, predominantly found in the Argentine Basin; small to medium-scale waves occur in both the Argentine Basin and the Hatteras Outer Ridge; and large-scale sediment waves are characteristic of the Hatteras Outer Ridge. In the Argentine Basin, variation in bottom-current flow velocity reveals relatively high flow velocities in the southern Zapiola region and lower velocities in northern Zapiola region. Sediment-wave asymmetry was further employed to determine flow directions, indicating north-westward flow in northern Zapiola, south-westward flow in southern Zapiola, and predominantly southward flow over the Hatteras Outer Ridge.
These findings demonstrate that sediment-wave morphology serves as a reliable proxy for interpreting bottom-current strength and flow direction in deep-marine environments. The observed spatial variations in sediment-wave characteristics reflect differences in oceanographic conditions between the Argentine Basin and the Hatteras Outer Ridge, contributing to a better understanding of contourite depositional systems and deep-ocean circulation.
